Redlining in Cowork

Yes, it works — three approaches

Cowork can handle redline-style comparison work. Three approaches: (1) paste both versions and ask for a diff, (2) upload docs and ask for clause-by-clause comparison, (3) comparison summaries — the smart play, where Claude highlights material differences and flags deviations from standard terms rather than producing a line-by-line redline.

Limitations
Slow on long documents. No native tracked-changes output in Cowork (it's not Word). Prompt injection risk from untrusted third-party docs — don't upload opposing counsel's marked-up drafts without awareness of this.

Exercises — 3 Group Builds

All three builds are done together as a group (live demo + follow along), with team discussion time after each. No individual exercises — everyone builds the same thing, then discusses within their team.

Build 1: Email Ghostwriter Validation

Claude learns your writing voice from sample emails. Frame as a validation exercise — "We're testing whether Claude can learn your voice, not replacing your writing." Everyone pastes in their own emails and tunes until it sounds like them.

15-min build + team discussion.

Build 2: Dashboard from Data Spreadsheets → Interactive

Turn spreadsheets into interactive dashboards. Live demo with sample data, then everyone follows along with their own data or provided samples.

15-min build + team discussion.

Build 3: Daily Brief Gmail + Slack → 4 Buckets

Pulls Gmail + Slack simultaneously. Classifies into: Overdue, Action Required, Active Contracts, FYI. Deduplicates across sources. Introduced after the ASK Ladder so participants understand the progression.

25-min build + team discussion.

Pre-Built Skills

4 skills built specifically for Ripple's legal team. Demo these during the session and deliver for them to install.

1. Email & Slack Summarizer

Two modes: Catchup (what happened + status) and Action Items (what you need to do). Special Group DM mode groups by topic, not chronology.

Triggers: "summarize this thread", "catch me up on", "what do I need to do"

2. Daily Brief

Pulls Gmail + Slack simultaneously. Classifies into: Overdue, Action Required, Active Contracts, FYI. Deduplicates across sources.

Triggers: "daily brief", "brief me", "what's on my plate today"

3. Counsel Brief Reviewer

Extracts 4 things from external counsel emails: Actions for Ripple, Questions from counsel, Needs business input, Counsel working on. Plus suggested reply points.

Triggers: "what does [lawyer] want us to do", "action items from this memo"

4. Contract History Summarizer

Full deal record: Agreements on file (by Ripple entity — RMA/RMDE/RPESA/BVI), Amendments, Deviations from standard terms, Open items.

Triggers: "What did we sign with [customer]?", "What are [customer]'s terms?"

Pro Tips

Builds are group, teams are for discussion
All 3 builds are done together — you demo, everyone follows along. Teams only come into play for the discussion portion after each build. This keeps the room in sync and avoids the chaos of 22 people going in different directions.
Interactive poll slides at the top
The opening has 3 interactive poll slides: "what prompt do you use?", "raise hand if weekly prompt", "raise hand if built a skill." These set the energy and give you a real-time read on the room's level. Don't skip them — they replace the old icebreaker.
The room dynamics (from today's prep call)
8 of 13 survey respondents use AI "regularly and it's changed how I work." But Cheryl flagged something important: there's internal pressure to use AI, so the survey is honest — nobody inflated their answers. The value is in Skills + Cowork + persistence — that's what they can't figure out alone. Don't spend too long on fundamentals.
Sensitivity — from Cheryl directly
"People might not want Sameer, our general counsel, to know that they don't know something." The breakout groups were deliberately designed so people feel safe. Don't put individuals on the spot in front of the whole room. Keep feedback within small groups. Some people will want to showboat — Cheryl wants to protect the less experienced from feeling bad.
Room setup (from today's call)
Everyone's in one big conference room with a large table. Each person on their own laptop + individual Zoom connection, mics off. You're on the big screen at the front. Questions come via Zoom chat — Cheryl will have team leads filter questions within groups so you don't get 5 duplicates. Teams will cluster in their seats.
The NDA prototype
One of Cheryl's team members already built an NDA review tool using a paralegal's playbook. It's a prototype ready to go live. Validate this publicly. Say "your team is already ahead of most organizations we work with." Then show how Skills formalize what they've started.
The compliance team
4 compliance people were added late. Suraj and Kasra are your secret weapons — they've already built tools in Claude Chat. Spread them across teams (they're in B and C). If anyone gets stuck during exercises, route them to Suraj or Kasra.
The Cowork gap
Cheryl flagged that the compliance team built amazing dashboards in Claude Chat but has to rebuild them every day because they're not using Cowork. This is your best "aha" moment. When you show Cowork vs Chat (slide 18), call this out directly: "Some of you have already experienced this pain."
The writing resistance
When Cheryl said "the challenge isn't putting things into writing," she was speaking for the room. Do not demo ghostwriting or email drafting. Every exercise is analysis/synthesis. The moment you show "summarize 50 emails into 3 action items" they'll get it — because that IS their pain.
Cheryl's dashboard vision (from today's call)
Cheryl and Sameer want a contract lifecycle dashboard: from request → first draft sent → negotiation turns → execution → go-live → revenue impact. The real insight: "What are the things we're all negotiating the same way? What clauses always change? Can Claude show us patterns across all our deals?" Tonight she sent 3 spreadsheets (IC agreements matrix, D&O, corporate structure) as data for Team D's breakout.
The ghostwriter came back
Despite the team's resistance to AI writing, Cheryl said on today's call: "The ghostwriter is a proof — it'll show you that this actually works because now it sounds like you. It allows us to validate the quality." She sees it as a validation exercise, not a writing exercise. If you do use it, frame it that way: "We're testing whether Claude can learn your voice, not replacing your writing."
